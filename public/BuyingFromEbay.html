Buying used computers safely on Ebay

Ebay is a risky place to buy high-value goods - some would say that it's impossible to buy a used computer safely on Ebay. I agree - you can never be completely safe when buying used goods from a stranger, which is basically what Ebay is about. But there are mechanisms in place to protect buyers under certain conditions, and I would still recommend Ebay. If you take a few key precautions to minimise the risk, in my experience there are significant advantages. I'm writing this article on a Toshiba laptop which I bought from Ebay 2 and a half years ago, and is still going strong. My wife's laptop was bought a few months later from Ebay, and works fine. I've bought several laptops for other people and language projects from Ebay, and had no significant problems with any of them! But I also take a lot of precautions before buying computers on Ebay (more about that below).

Why buy a used computer from Ebay?

  • Price. On Ebay it's not hard to find a 1-2 year old laptop for 50-75% of the new price. Some still have a manufacturer's warranty.
  • Choice. Laptop models change every few months, so Ebay can help if you want a particular model, which has been discontinued. 
  • Standardisation. If you're in charge of a network, it makes life much easier if you buy identical computers - that way, you can clone multiple workstations using Ghost or Acronis, and hardware support becomes much easier.
Say you buy one or two new laptops for a language project, you set them up and decide that you really like this model, for whatever reasons - build quality, battery life, keeps cool in the heat, etc. A few months later, when you come to buy more laptops, you're not going to find the same model new anymore, so Ebay is the obvious place to look. The alternative is to buy a new laptop, often a model which you have never used, or even seen before. You take a considerable risk when you do this, unless you have a recommendation from someone whose judgement you trust. Will the model you choose be sturdy and reliable, or will it turn out to have some significant weakness? All laptop makers turn out a few lemons, even our old SIL favourites Toshiba and Dell - for example, laptops whose lids start cracking after a few months, mouse buttons which come off and don't press down properly, components that overheat easily, and other hardware & build quality problems. It doesn't always follow that the cheaper models are the problematic ones, either.

Important precautions to take

  • If a seller has not sold several laptops before and received excellent feedback for them, I would not buy from them. You can easily check a seller's sales history; it's now included as part of the feedback profile. 
  • If they have genuine-sounding negative feedback comments from buyers, I would not buy from them.
  • If their feedback score is less than about 20, I would not buy from them. There are plenty of fraudsters who set up multiple accounts, and try to "sell" goods which they don't actually have - they just lift the photos and descriptions straight off the Internet. When the account gets too much negative feedback, they just abandon it.
  • If the listing doesn't explicitly state "excellent condition", "perfect working order" or the like, I would contact them them and check it before
    considering buying from them.
  • I would only ever pay with PayPal - you have no protection if you pay through bank transfer or Western Union and the like.
  • Check that the seller will ship to the place you want - for example, many sellers will not send to an address that is not confirmed with PayPal. Some will be flexible about this if you contact them ahead of time...
  • If anything in the listing makes me suspicious, I would pray about it, and if I still felt uneasy, I would not buy from them.

And a few general Ebay tips...

  • If you want to bid on an auction listing (as opposed to a "Buy It Now"), wait until the last minute before the auction ends if possible, then bid your top price. Many people wait until the last minute to bid, so the price often increases a lot at the very end. If you bid earlier, the final price is likely to be pushed higher.
  • Most items end in the late afternoon and evening, local time, and these tend to attract the most bids. If you find an item which ends in the morning or early afternoon, it's likely to finish cheaper.
  • Misspelt items often attract much fewer bids - try http://www.fatfingers.co.uk to search for common misspellings. But be aware that the guy whose "Top of the range Tohsiba laptop" ends for $10 may not honour his contract to sell it at that price (would you? :-))
